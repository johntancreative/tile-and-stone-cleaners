What is Porcelain?

Porcelain is a type of ceramic tile made from a refined clay mixture that is fired at temperatures exceeding 1,200 degrees Celsius. This extreme firing process creates a tile that is exceptionally dense, hard, and resistant to moisture absorption. Porcelain tiles typically have a water absorption rate of less than 0.5%, making them significantly more waterproof than standard ceramic tiles.

Porcelain resists chemicals, stains, and thermal shock better than most other tile materials. It can handle pressure cleaning at up to 1,300 PSI when the grout is sound and in good condition, making it one of the few tile types that can withstand high-pressure cleaning without risk of surface damage. This durability makes porcelain a popular choice for both indoor and outdoor applications.

Types of Porcelain Tiles

Not all porcelain tiles are the same. The three main types differ in their surface treatment, porosity, and maintenance requirements. Understanding which type you have is essential for proper cleaning and sealing.

1

Natural (Unpolished)

Has a textured, matte surface with higher porosity than other types. Natural porcelain is more prone to absorbing stains and moisture, making regular sealing essential to maintain its appearance and protect the surface.

2

Polished

Features a glossy, reflective surface achieved through mechanical polishing after firing. Polished porcelain is often pre-sealed at the factory but may still require additional sealing depending on the manufacturer and usage.

3

Glazed

Coated with a glass-like glaze applied before the final firing. Glazed porcelain is the most stain-resistant type with very low water absorption. The glaze creates a protective barrier that repels moisture and resists most household chemicals.

Porcelain vs Ceramic

While porcelain is technically a type of ceramic, it differs significantly in composition, durability, and performance. Understanding these differences helps you choose the right tile for your space and maintain it correctly.

Porcelain

  • Made from denser, more refined clay fired at higher temperatures (1,200+ degrees Celsius)
  • Water absorption rate of just 0.5% -- significantly more waterproof
  • Superior scratch resistance due to higher density and hardness
  • Suitable for both indoor and outdoor use, including frost-prone areas
  • More resistant to chemicals, stains, and heavy foot traffic
More Durable

Ceramic

  • Made from coarser clay fired at lower temperatures (900-1,100 degrees Celsius)
  • Higher water absorption rate -- more porous and prone to moisture damage
  • Softer surface that scratches and chips more easily over time
  • Best suited for indoor use in low-traffic areas like walls and splashbacks
  • More affordable upfront but may require more frequent replacement

Porcelain Tile Care

Regular maintenance keeps your porcelain tiles looking new and extends the lifespan of both the tiles and grout. Follow these five essential care steps to protect your porcelain surfaces between professional services.

1

Sweep Regularly

Remove loose dirt, dust, and grit with a soft broom or vacuum. Grit left on the surface acts like sandpaper underfoot, gradually dulling the finish and creating microscopic scratches.

2

Clean Grout Lines

Grout is more porous than porcelain and absorbs stains quickly. Clean grout lines regularly with a pH-neutral cleaner and a stiff brush to prevent discolouration and mould growth.

3

Wash Wall Tiles

Bathroom and kitchen wall tiles accumulate soap scum, grease, and water spots. Wipe them down weekly with a non-abrasive cleaner to prevent buildup that becomes harder to remove over time.

4

Keep Bathroom Grout Dry

After showering, wipe down tiles and grout with a squeegee or dry cloth. Standing moisture promotes mould and mildew growth in grout lines, leading to staining and deterioration.

5

Seal Grout Lines

Apply a penetrating grout sealer every 12-18 months to protect grout from moisture absorption and staining. Sealed grout is easier to clean and resists mould growth significantly better than unsealed grout.

Daily Cleaning Tips for Porcelain

Small daily habits make the biggest difference in keeping porcelain tiles looking pristine. These five simple practices prevent the buildup of dirt and grime that leads to costly professional intervention.

1

Dust Mop Daily

Use a microfiber dust mop to pick up fine particles that a broom might miss. Microfiber traps dust rather than pushing it around, keeping your porcelain surface clean and scratch-free.

2

Mats at Entrances

Place doormats at every entrance to trap grit, sand, and dirt before it reaches your porcelain floors. This single step prevents the majority of surface scratching caused by foot traffic.

3

Shower Ventilation

Run the exhaust fan during and for 15 minutes after each shower. Proper ventilation reduces humidity that causes mould and mildew to form on porcelain tiles and grout in wet areas.

4

Mildew-Resistant Caulk

Use mildew-resistant silicone caulk around shower edges, bath surrounds, and tile transitions. This prevents black mould from taking hold in areas where grout meets other surfaces.

5

Mop 2-3x Weekly

Damp mop porcelain floors two to three times per week with warm water and a pH-neutral tile cleaner. Avoid excessive water -- a lightly damp mop is sufficient. Too much water can seep into grout lines and cause damage over time.

Sealing Porcelain Tiles

Not all porcelain tiles require sealing. Glazed porcelain tiles have a built-in glass-like coating that makes them virtually impervious to moisture and stains -- sealing glazed porcelain is unnecessary and can even cause issues by trapping moisture on the surface.

However, natural (unpolished) and polished porcelain varieties are more porous and benefit significantly from sealing. For these types, water-based penetrating sealers are recommended for grout lines, while impregnating sealers work best on porous tile surfaces. These sealers penetrate into the material and create an invisible barrier that repels water and oil-based stains without altering the tile's appearance.

Always test sealer on a small, inconspicuous area first. Some sealers can darken or alter the colour of certain porcelain finishes. Your Tile and Stone Cleaners technician will assess your specific tiles and recommend the appropriate sealer type and application method.

Grout Cleaning Methods

Grout is the weakest link in any tiled surface. Even the most durable porcelain tiles can look dirty and neglected when grout lines become stained and discoloured. Here are two proven methods for cleaning grout at home between professional services.

Bleach Solution (50/50)

Mix equal parts household bleach and warm water. Apply to grout lines with a stiff brush, scrub thoroughly, and let sit for 5-10 minutes before rinsing completely with clean water.

  • Effective at killing mould and mildew on contact
  • Whitens and brightens stained grout quickly
  • Requires proper ventilation -- open windows and use a fan
  • Always wear rubber gloves and eye protection
  • Rinse thoroughly to prevent residue buildup

Hydrogen Peroxide

Apply 3% hydrogen peroxide directly to grout lines. Let it fizz and work for 10-15 minutes, then scrub with a nylon brush and rinse with clean water.

  • Odourless and gentler than bleach -- no harsh fumes
  • Safe for use around children and pets once dry
  • Effective at lifting organic stains and light mould
  • Won't discolour or damage porcelain tile surfaces
  • Can be mixed with baking soda for a paste on stubborn stains
Gentle Option
