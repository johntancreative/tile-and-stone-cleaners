What is Limestone?

Limestone is a sedimentary rock formed over millions of years from the accumulation of material on the ocean floor. It is composed primarily of calcium carbonate, derived from the shells, bones, and skeletal fragments of marine organisms. Through sustained heat and immense pressure deep within the earth, this sediment gradually transforms into solid rock.

Many limestone surfaces contain visible fossilised marine material, giving each piece a unique natural history. Limestone is found in a range of common colours including black, grey, white, yellow, and brown, making it a versatile choice for interior and exterior flooring, wall cladding, and decorative surfaces.

Cleaning Limestone Floors

Limestone is a porous natural stone that is vulnerable to scratches, stains, and fractures. It needs to breathe -- meaning you cannot use synthetic sealers or urethane coatings that trap moisture within the stone. Instead, we use impregnating sealers that penetrate deeply into the limestone's pores, allowing the stone to release moisture naturally while remaining protected.

Foot traffic is the primary cause of dullness in limestone floors. Over time, dust, sand, and grit tracked in from outside accumulates on the surface. These fine particles act like sandpaper underfoot, creating thousands of microscopic scratches that eventually cover large portions of the floor, dulling the finish and causing the stone to lose its reflective properties.

Older buildings often feature chalky, porous French and Spanish limestone varieties that are more prone to staining than marble. These softer limestones absorb oils and liquids quickly and can be etched by acidic substances such as lemon juice, wine, or cleaning products containing acid.

When cleaning limestone, only use neutral pH detergents. Never pressure clean limestone above 800 PSI, and only if the grout is in good condition. Excessive pressure can blast out grout, force water deep into the stone, and cause surface damage that is expensive to repair.

Limestone looks best with a honed or low-to-medium sheen finish. Some low-calcite limestone varieties are difficult to polish and may require vitrification -- a chemical hardening process that creates a polishable surface where the natural stone cannot achieve one on its own.

Advantages & Disadvantages of Limestone

Limestone is a popular natural stone for flooring and surfaces, but it comes with trade-offs. Understanding both sides helps you make informed decisions about installation and maintenance.

Advantages

  • Exceptionally durable -- withstands high foot traffic and daily use when properly maintained
  • Easy to clean and maintain with the right products and routine
  • Less expensive than marble or granite, offering natural stone beauty at a lower price point
  • Naturally moisture resistant, making it a good choice for bathrooms and kitchens when sealed correctly

Disadvantages

  • Feels cold and hard underfoot without underfloor heating
  • Requires regular sealing to maintain its protective barrier against stains and moisture
  • Sensitive to acidic substances -- prolonged contact with acids causes etching and surface damage over time

Sealing Limestone Tiles

Sealing is essential for protecting your limestone investment. There are two main types of sealers used on limestone, each with different properties, finishes, and lifespans. Choosing the right one depends on your stone type, location, and the level of protection required.

Impregnating Sealer

  • Deep penetrating -- reacts chemically with the stone's pores for lasting protection
  • Available in transparent or colour-enhancing finish options
  • Uses nano-technology to create an invisible protective barrier within the stone
  • Shields against both water and oil-based stains
  • Makes ongoing cleaning and maintenance significantly easier
  • Prevents stain absorption before it happens
Recommended

Topical Sealer

  • Creates a protective coating on the surface of the stone
  • Available in gloss, satin, or matte finish options
  • Protects from external factors including foot traffic and spills
  • Does not penetrate like an impregnating sealer -- sits on top
  • Approximate lifespan of 3 years before reapplication is needed

Pre-Sealing Preparation

Proper preparation before sealing is critical. Rushing this step leads to trapped stains, sealer failure, and costly rework. Follow these essential steps before any sealer is applied to limestone.

1

Clean & Condition

Clean all surfaces and achieve the desired condition before sealing. Stains may become impossible to remove once sealed over.

2

Avoid Acid Cleaners

Never use acidic cleaners on limestone at any stage. Acid causes irreversible etching and chemical damage to the calcium carbonate surface.

3

Remove Old Coatings

Strip and remove any existing coatings, wax, or old topical sealers before applying new sealer. Old layers prevent proper adhesion and penetration.

4

Ensure Complete Dryness

The floor must be completely dry before applying any sealer. Moisture trapped beneath sealer causes white hazing, peeling, and premature failure.

Protection from Acid Etching

Limestone and all calcite-based natural stones -- including marble and travertine -- are particularly prone to acid etching. This occurs when acidic substances come into contact with the calcium carbonate in the stone, causing a chemical reaction that dissolves the surface layer and leaves dull, rough marks.

Common household substances that damage limestone include lemon juice, cola, wine, champagne, vinegar, and many bathroom cleaning products. Even brief contact with these liquids can leave visible etch marks on polished limestone surfaces.

A honed (matte) finish is a popular choice for limestone flooring specifically because it shows fewer etch marks than a polished finish. On a glossy polished surface, even minor etching is immediately visible. On a honed surface, light etching blends in more naturally with the stone's existing texture.

Acid Warning: Always clean acidic spills on limestone immediately. Lemon juice, wine, cola, champagne, and vinegar will etch and permanently damage limestone surfaces on contact. Keep acidic cleaning products away from all calcite-based natural stones. If etching has already occurred, professional honing and re-polishing may be required to restore the surface.

Limestone Care & Maintenance

Proper daily care extends the life of your limestone surfaces and reduces the frequency of professional restoration. Follow these guidelines to keep your limestone looking its best between professional services.

Do's

  • Clean spills immediately to prevent staining and etching
  • Seal limestone floors with appropriate stone-specific sealers
  • Place a mat at every entrance to trap grit and sand
  • Use a microfiber mop for regular cleaning
  • Clean regularly with neutral pH stone-safe detergents

Don'ts

  • Never use bleach, ammonia, or vinegar on limestone
  • Don't use acidic cleaners of any kind on the stone surface
  • Never use abrasive cleaners or scouring pads -- they scratch and dull the finish

2

Cleaning & Sealing

For dulled limestone surfaces, new installations, or worn sealant. We restore the natural shine and character of your limestone before applying protective sealer to guard against future damage and staining.

3

Tile & Grout Cleaning

Specialised limestone tile and grout cleaning using professional-grade equipment including van-mounted high-pressure systems. We extract embedded dirt, grime, and discolouration from both tile faces and grout lines.

4

Tile Sealing

Seal porous limestone tiles to prevent staining and moisture damage. Our sealers create a protective barrier within the stone that repels water and oil-based contaminants while allowing the stone to breathe naturally.

5

Stripping

Remove old sealant, wax buildup, and surface residue to restore your limestone's original appearance. Proper stripping is essential before any new sealer application to ensure adhesion and even coverage.

Grinding

Smooth out surface imperfections and open a new layer of stone for a honed or polished finish. Grinding removes deep scratches, stubborn stains, and corrects lippage -- uneven tile edges caused by shifting or poor installation.

Honing

Achieve a satin-feel, smooth, velvety limestone surface. Honed finishes require less maintenance than polished surfaces and suit casual living environments. The matte texture shows fewer marks and etch spots than a glossy polish.

8

Polishing

Fine-grade diamond discs create a reflective, mirror-like finish on your limestone. Polishing removes invisible surface scratches, allowing the stone's natural crystals to reflect light. The resulting smooth surface has smaller pores that collect less dirt.

9

Efflorescence Treatment

Remove unsightly salt deposit buildup that appears as white powdery residue on limestone surfaces. We use specialised equipment and chemicals to dissolve and extract efflorescence without damaging the underlying stone.

10

Minor Tile Repair

Fix cracked, chipped, loose, and discoloured limestone tiles in both indoor and outdoor settings. Minor repairs prevent further damage and extend the lifespan of your limestone installation without the cost of full replacement.

11

Grout Colour Sealing

Re-colour stained, discoloured grout lines or alter the existing grout colour to refresh the overall appearance of your limestone floor. Colour sealing provides a uniform look while simultaneously protecting the grout from future staining and moisture damage.
