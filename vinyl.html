What is Vinyl Flooring?

Vinyl is a synthetic plastic flooring material specifically designed for high-traffic areas. It is commonly found in shopping malls, nursing homes, hospitals, kitchens, laundries, and commercial spaces where durability and easy maintenance are essential. Vinyl flooring is made from a combination of cork powder, wood dust, pigments, and PVC (polyvinyl chloride), creating a resilient surface that withstands heavy daily use.

Vinyl flooring is one of the most affordable and practical flooring options available. It is waterproof, resistant to staining, and comfortable underfoot. Modern vinyl comes in a wide range of designs that can convincingly replicate the appearance of timber, stone, and ceramic tiles at a fraction of the cost.

Despite its durability, vinyl flooring requires regular professional maintenance to maintain its appearance. Over time, the surface polish wears away due to foot traffic, cleaning chemicals, and general use, leaving the floor looking dull and tired. Professional stripping and resealing restores the protective polish layer and brings back the floor's original shine.

What is Linoleum?

Linoleum is a natural flooring material that was first patented in 1863. Unlike vinyl, which is synthetic, linoleum is made from solidified linseed oil combined with natural resins, cork powder, wood flour, and limestone. These natural ingredients are pressed onto a jute or canvas backing to create a durable, resilient flooring surface.

One of linoleum's unique properties is that it actually strengthens over time as the linseed oil in the material continues to oxidise and harden. This makes linoleum one of the few flooring materials that becomes more durable with age rather than degrading. A well-maintained linoleum floor can last 40 years or more.

Linoleum is naturally anti-bacterial, biodegradable, and hypoallergenic, making it an environmentally responsible flooring choice. It is commonly found in schools, hospitals, and homes where indoor air quality and sustainability are important considerations.

Cleaning & Sealing Vinyl and Linoleum

Professional vinyl and linoleum floor care involves the application of non-toxic finishing agents that protect the surface and restore its original shine. We recommend reapplying polish at least twice yearly for floors in high-traffic areas to maintain optimal appearance and protection.

For freshly stripped floors that have been taken back to bare material, a minimum of three layers of polish must be applied. Each layer builds on the previous one, creating a durable, multi-coat protective barrier that resists scuffing, scratching, and staining from daily foot traffic.

1

Preparatory Cleaning

Deep clean the floor surface to remove all dirt, grime, old polish residue, and contaminants. This step ensures the new polish adheres properly and creates a uniform, streak-free finish.

2

Buffing & Scrubbing

Industrial buffing and scrubbing machines prepare the surface for polish application. Buffing smooths any imperfections and creates an ideal surface for the polish to bond with.

3

Traffic-Resistant Polish

Apply multiple coats of traffic-resistant polish designed for vinyl and linoleum surfaces. Each coat is allowed to dry before the next is applied, building a durable, high-gloss protective layer.

Maintenance Tip: Between professional services, maintain your vinyl or linoleum floors by damp mopping with a pH-neutral cleaner 2-3 times per week. Avoid using abrasive cleaners, steel wool, or harsh chemicals that can strip the polish and damage the surface. Place mats at entrances to trap grit that causes scratching, and wipe up spills promptly to prevent staining.
